# Cross-Company Correlations — Q4 2025 Earnings

> **Companies covered:** Apple (Q1 FY2026, Jan 29), Microsoft (Q2 FY2026, Jan 29), Alphabet (Q4 2025, Feb 4), Amazon (Q4 2025, Feb 5)
> **Sectors covered:** Cloud infrastructure, Digital advertising, Semiconductors

---

## Shared Themes

### 1. Universal Revenue Beats, but Stock Reactions Driven by Capital Allocation — Not Operations

Every company in this cycle beat on revenue and EPS. Apple beat by +4.6% on revenue and +6.8% on EPS. Alphabet beat by +2.4% on revenue and +7.6% on EPS. Amazon beat on every revenue and operating income line. Microsoft beat on Intelligent Cloud by nearly $2B. Despite this, three of the four companies saw their stock decline in the days or weeks following the print. The market's attention was entirely on forward capex disclosures, not trailing operational performance.

This marks a structural break from prior earnings cycles. In 2021–2023, beats were rewarded proportionally. In Q4 2025, beats were treated as background noise. The signal that mattered was whether management's capital commitment exceeded investor models — and for Amazon and Alphabet, it did so by $53B and $55–65B respectively.

**Non-obvious read:** The disconnect between operational excellence and stock performance is actually a vote of confidence in the long-term thesis. A sell-off on excellent earnings implies that investors expect the business to continue performing and are concerned about ROI timeline — not whether the businesses are working.

---

### 2. "Supply Constrained" — A Coordinated Signal Across All Three Hyperscalers

CEO Sundar Pichai (Alphabet), Amazon management, and Microsoft executives all independently used functionally identical language: AI demand is **supply constrained, not demand constrained**. The bottleneck is data center capacity, not enterprise willingness to pay. This phrase appearing across all three Q4 reports — companies that compete directly with each other for enterprise workloads — amounts to an industry-wide data point that cannot be attributed to competitive framing.

The practical implication: the hyperscalers would spend more if they could. The $495–640B in combined 2026 capex guidance is not an aggressive investment thesis by management — it is their honest assessment of how much capacity they can bring online given real-world constraints (power infrastructure, fab capacity, construction timelines).

---

### 3. AI Revenue Is Real, but Deliberately Opaque

None of the four companies disclosed an explicit AI revenue line. This is coordinated non-disclosure, and the reasons are strategic:

- **Alphabet**: AI Overviews are embedded in Search (+17%); breaking out "AI Search revenue" would invite regulatory scrutiny and competitive reverse-engineering of pricing
- **Amazon**: Trainium + Graviton ARR of $10B+ was the most specific disclosure in the cycle, but still bundled across chip types and not isolated to AI
- **Microsoft**: Described the AI business as "larger than some established franchises" — quantifying without quantifying
- **Apple**: No AI revenue attributed to Apple Intelligence at all; the entire bet is on ecosystem engagement and upgrade cycle acceleration

The first company to disclose granular AI revenue will likely receive a positive multiple re-rating. The others will then face pressure to follow. This is the single most under-discussed dynamic in the earnings season: the gap between what the market believes these AI businesses are worth and the actual disclosed revenue remains entirely faith-based.

---

### 4. Cloud Backlog Convergence — AWS and Google Are Mirror Images

The most striking convergence in raw data: AWS reported a $244B order backlog (+40% YoY, +22% QoQ). Google Cloud reported a $240B backlog (+55% sequential). Two competitors, fighting directly for the same enterprise AI workloads, with backlogs within $4B of each other — roughly a 1.6% difference. This is not coincidence. It reflects:

1. Enterprise procurement teams are hedging across providers (multi-cloud commitments)
2. Both providers are winning similarly-sized deals from the same finite pool of Fortune 500 AI spenders
3. The AI cloud market is large enough to support two roughly equal backlog leaders simultaneously

Azure does not disclose a comparable backlog figure, which is itself informative — Microsoft's revenue visibility flows through its installed base of 400M+ commercial Office seats rather than cloud contract commitments.

---

### 5. The Anthropic Axis — A Structural Conflict Hidden in Plain Sight

Both Amazon and Alphabet are significant investors in Anthropic. Amazon's $4B investment led to Project Rainier — a 500,000+ chip cluster of Trainium2 chips operated specifically to train Claude models. Google Cloud's $240B backlog includes a landmark deal with Anthropic (access to 1 million TPU chips). Two hyperscalers that directly compete for enterprise cloud workloads are jointly funding the AI lab that is, in turn, driving demand for both of their cloud infrastructures.

The conflict is structural: if Anthropic's Claude models are the best enterprise AI option (which is a mainstream view entering 2026), then both AWS and Google Cloud benefit from Claude adoption — regardless of which provider hosts the workload. The Anthropic relationship is effectively a demand co-creation vehicle for both, which explains why both companies are willing to continue investing even though they compete head-to-head in cloud sales cycles.

## Key Divergences

### 1. Apple's Fundamentally Different Position in the AI Economy

Apple is the only company among the four that is a **net beneficiary** of the AI infrastructure buildout rather than a net spender. While Amazon, Alphabet, and Microsoft are collectively committing $495–640B in 2026 capex, Apple's capex remains a fraction of that. Apple benefits from the AI cycle through:

- **TSMC allocation**: TSMC's advanced node utilization (77% at N3+) is strengthened by hyperscaler AI chip demand, which funds the fab capacity that Apple's A-series chips also use
- **Services monetization**: App Store billings increase as AI-powered apps (coding assistants, writing tools, image generators) become the highest-grossing category
- **Upgrade cycle**: Apple Intelligence as the upgrade catalyst — every new iPhone must be capable of on-device AI inference, creating a hardware upgrade cycle driven by AI capability thresholds

Apple's Services segment ($30B in Q4, all-time record) is the closest analog to a high-margin AI business among the four companies — but it is platform monetization rather than infrastructure investment.

The divergence in capital intensity is dramatic: Apple's gross margin expanded to 48.2%; Amazon's projected FCF in 2026 turns negative. This is not a reflection of business quality differences — it reflects entirely different positions in the AI value chain.

---

### 2. Capex Shock Asymmetry — Microsoft vs. Amazon and Alphabet

Microsoft guided to $120B+ in 2026 capex and its stock reacted positively. Amazon guided to $200B and fell -8% to -11%. Alphabet guided to $175–185B and fell -7% to -10%. The operational results were broadly similar in quality. The difference is entirely in how the guidance was communicated:

- **Microsoft** had been progressively telegraphing AI infrastructure commitment over multiple prior quarters. The $120B+ was a continuation, not a surprise. Azure's +38% CC growth gave investors confidence in demand validation before seeing the capex number.
- **Amazon and Alphabet** both disclosed outsized capex guidance that was **50–100% above prior Street consensus** in a single announcement. The market had not been primed. The shock magnitude — not the absolute dollar amount — drove the sell-off.

This is an important lesson for capital markets communication: the same $55B capex beat, delivered across 4 quarters as gradual increases, would likely have been absorbed without a sell-off. Concentrated surprises are penalized disproportionately relative to their actual information content.

---

### 3. Margin Structure Divergence — 43% vs. 20%

Cloud segment operating margins reveal fundamentally different business maturities:

| Provider | Cloud Op. Margin | Margin Trend |
|----------|-----------------|--------------|
| Microsoft Intelligent Cloud | ~43% | Stable/slight expansion |
| AWS | ~35% | Expanding (+0.4 pts QoQ) |
| Google Cloud | ~20% | Expanding rapidly (+85% YoY income) |

Microsoft's 43% margin reflects a software-heavy architecture where Azure revenue is complemented by Windows Server, SQL Server, and GitHub — all near-zero variable cost products. AWS's 35% reflects infrastructure economics with a maturing operating leverage curve. Google Cloud's 20% reflects a business that only turned profitable in 2023 and is now rapidly compressing losses into margin.

By 2027–2028, Google Cloud's margin trajectory implies convergence toward 30–35% — which, at its growth rate, would represent a dramatic increase in absolute operating income. The divergence today is a timing artifact, not a structural quality difference.

---

### 4. The Revenue Quality Paradox in Advertising

Both Alphabet (+13.5% total ads) and Amazon (+23% ads) delivered outstanding advertising results in Q4 2025. Both companies' stocks declined following earnings. This creates a paradox: how can excellent advertising businesses be a source of investor concern?

The answer is that the advertising results were treated as confirmation that the core businesses are healthy — which validates the capex investment thesis — while simultaneously being ignored as near-term value drivers because the magnitude of capital commitment overshadowed any near-term cash generation.

Separately, the divergence within advertising is analytically important: Google Search (+17%) significantly outpaced Google Network (-1.5%) within the same company. Amazon Ads (+23%) outpaced Amazon's total revenue growth (+14%). These differentials indicate that commercial intent-based, first-party data-linked advertising is growing substantially faster than open-web programmatic — a divergence that will compound over time.

---

### 5. AI Monetization Timeline Divergence

The four companies are at different stages of converting AI investment to revenue:

- **Microsoft**: Most advanced at revenue disclosure; Azure AI is "larger than some established franchises," Copilot seats adding incremental revenue to existing M365 licenses
- **Amazon**: Most specific on chip economics; Trainium + Graviton $10B+ ARR is the clearest AI revenue number in the cycle
- **Alphabet**: Most aggressive on efficiency metrics (78% Gemini serving cost reduction) but least specific on AI revenue contribution; the cloud backlog ($240B) is the proxy
- **Apple**: No AI revenue disclosed; the monetization is through ecosystem effects that will take 3–5 years to fully manifest in Services revenue

### Custom Silicon — The Hidden Competitive Arms Race

The publicly discussed arms race is between hyperscalers for cloud market share. The less-discussed race is hyperscalers versus NVIDIA for control of AI chip economics.

All three hyperscalers reached material custom silicon deployment milestones in Q4 2025:

| Chip | Provider | Process | Status | Performance Claim |
|------|----------|---------|--------|-------------------|
| TPU Ironwood (7th gen) | Google | TSMC N3 | Production; 58% custom AI market share | — |
| Trainium3 | Amazon | TSMC 3nm | Production; sold out through mid-2026 | — |
| Maia 200 | Microsoft | — | Deployed Azure data centers Jan 2026 | 3x FP4 vs. Trainium3 |

Custom ASIC shipments are projected to grow +44.6% in 2026 versus +16.1% for NVIDIA GPUs. This is not displacement for training workloads — NVIDIA's CUDA ecosystem remains essential for frontier model training. But for inference — where workloads are more predictable and efficiency matters more than flexibility — ASICs are structurally advantaged. Since inference is the high-volume, high-frequency operation (every API call, every search query, every Copilot completion), custom silicon economics compound over time.

The strategic implication: NVIDIA's revenue growth will continue, but its share of total AI compute dollars will compress even as total AI spending surges. Hyperscalers are simultaneously their best customer and their most formidable long-term competitor.

---

### Who Is Spending Most Aggressively?

On absolute dollars: **Amazon** ($200B). On magnitude of surprise: **Alphabet** (+98% vs. estimates). On execution risk: **Amazon**, which simultaneously faces negative projected FCF from the commitment.

Amazon's aggression is qualitatively different from Alphabet's or Microsoft's. Amazon issued a EUR 14.5B bond to fund the buildout — debt financing AI infrastructure is a bet that cloud cash flows will service the debt well before the capital depreciates. Alphabet is funding from existing cash generation. Microsoft is funding primarily from its $200B+ revenue base and existing free cash flow.

The signal from Amazon's debt issuance is worth noting: Amazon believed its equity investors would react negatively to the capex magnitude (correct — stock fell -11%) but that bond investors would accept the credit risk (also correct). This implies Amazon's management expected — and accepted — a near-term equity valuation hit as a cost of securing long-term infrastructure position.

## Sector Ripple Effects

### Semiconductor Demand Outlook

The Q4 2025 earnings cycle was the most consequential demand signal for semiconductors in years. The aggregate capex of $620–640B across big tech, combined with the "supply constrained" framing, validated the AI infrastructure supercycle thesis and drove the SOX index to a 12-month return of +52.0%.

**TSMC's unique position**: TSMC is the single point of exposure to every major demand vector simultaneously — NVIDIA (Blackwell, N3), Apple (A19, N3P), Google (TPU Ironwood, N3), Amazon (Trainium3, N3), Broadcom (XPUs, N3+N2), and Micron (HBM logic base dies). The company's Q1 2026 revenue guidance of $34.6–35.8B (+38% YoY) is the downstream confirmation of everything the big tech earnings season implied. Apple's iPhone miss (Mac -6.7%) and Wearables miss both cited TSMC advanced-node allocation constraints — meaning even Apple cannot get all the silicon it wants at N3.

**The HBM supercycle**: Memory is the sleeper story of the earnings season. Every GPU cluster — whether NVIDIA Blackwell, Amazon Trainium, Google TPU, or Microsoft Maia — requires High Bandwidth Memory. The combined demand from hyperscaler capex programs has sold out SK Hynix and Micron HBM capacity through all of calendar 2026. Micron reported FQ1 2026 gross margins exceeding 50%, up from ~22% in FY2024 — a margin expansion unprecedented in the memory industry. The $440B+ global memory market projection for 2026 flows directly from the capex guidance disclosed in this earnings cycle.

**Custom silicon vs. NVIDIA**: NVIDIA reported record Q4 FY2026 revenues of $68.1B (+73% YoY) and the stock fell 5.46%. The market is pricing in share erosion in the incremental dollar, even as the total market grows. This is the correct analytical frame — NVIDIA's CUDA moat is structural for training, but inference economics favor custom ASICs, and inference is the volume play.

### Cloud Market Evolution

The Q4 2025 results establish that the cloud optimization cycle (2022–2024) is fully absorbed. Enterprise spending has re-entered the active phase, now AI-led rather than migration-led. Key structural dynamics:

**The speed hierarchy**: Google Cloud (+48%) > Azure (+29%, +38% CC) > AWS (+24%). The fastest-growing provider is also the smallest in absolute terms and the most aggressively priced — which is why the growth rate is not the right ranking metric for investors. AWS's $35.6B quarter with $244B backlog and 35% margins is the most valuable cloud business even if it grows at the slowest rate.

**Multi-cloud as the default**: 85%+ of enterprises now operate multi-cloud. The strategic implication: AWS's and Google's near-identical $240–244B backlogs are not competing for the same enterprise commitment — they are co-existing within the same enterprise architectures. The zero-sum framing of cloud competition is outdated.

**AI as the inflection**: 94% of enterprises use at least one cloud service. The new variable is which AI platform they commit to — and AI commitments are stickier than infrastructure commitments because they involve fine-tuned models, proprietary data pipelines, and workforce retraining. The hyperscaler that wins the AI commitment likely holds the enterprise for a decade, not a contract cycle.

---

### Digital Advertising Health

The advertising results provide a clean read on digital ad ecosystem health, largely because the capex-driven stock declines obscured genuinely excellent results:

**Search is structurally resilient**: Google Search +17% is among the strongest quarters in recent history, and it came despite — or arguably because of — AI Overviews. The thesis that generative AI would cannibalize search monetization has not materialized. AI Overviews are expanding query volume for complex, multi-step commercial intent queries that were previously below the monetizable threshold.

**Retail media is the fastest structural grower**: Amazon Advertising at +23% YoY grew faster than every other major ad segment. At $21.3B quarterly ($85B+ annualized run rate), Amazon Advertising is now the third-largest digital ad business globally. Its closed-loop attribution — connecting ad impression to verified purchase — is the moat that makes it defensible against Google's and Meta's scale advantages.

**The walled garden consolidation is complete**: Google Network (-1.5%), the only open-web programmatic segment among the four companies, declined. This confirms the secular structural shift: first-party identity-linked inventory (Google Search, YouTube, Amazon, Meta) is growing; open-web programmatic is contracting. The transition was predicted for years and is now unambiguously the data.

**AI is the advertising infrastructure layer**: Performance Max, Amazon's bid models, and Meta's Advantage+ are driving CPM recovery by making targeting more precise and campaigns more efficient. AI in advertising is not disrupting the industry — it is making the dominant incumbents more dominant, by increasing their lead in targeting quality over alternatives that lack equivalent first-party data.

| Company | Earnings Date | Stock Move (post) | Primary Catalyst |
|---------|--------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Apple | Jan 29, 2026 | +1.19% (day of) | Clean beat; no capex shock; China upside |
| Microsoft | Jan 29, 2026 | Modestly positive | Azure reacceleration; capex absorbed |
| Alphabet | Feb 4, 2026 | -7% to -10% (following week) | $175–185B capex guidance (+$55–65B shock) |
| Amazon | Feb 5, 2026 | -8% to -11% (day after); -15% peak | $200B capex guidance (+$53B shock) |

The contrast between Apple/Microsoft (Jan 29) and Alphabet/Amazon (Feb 4–5) created a sentiment divergence across the earnings window. January 29 anchored positive large-cap tech sentiment; the following week's hyperscaler capex disclosures destabilized it.

**The January 29 effect**: Apple and Microsoft reporting the same day produced a dual-beat that briefly anchored positive sentiment for the entire earnings season. Both companies told a story of strong execution with manageable capital commitments. Investors who extrapolated that framing to Alphabet and Amazon — expected to report days later — were caught by the capex shock.

---

### Correlated Reactions — The Capex Contagion

Amazon's $200B capex disclosure (Feb 5) created immediate read-across selling pressure on Alphabet's stock, which had already declined on its own capex guide the day before. The sequential timing — Alphabet on Feb 4, Amazon on Feb 5 — amplified the effect: by Feb 5, the market had absorbed two consecutive nights of hyperscaler capex shocks. The combined effect was a sell-off across the entire large-cap tech complex, even in companies (Microsoft, Apple) whose own capex postures had not changed.

This correlation reveals an underappreciated risk factor: **hyperscaler capex guidance is now a correlated market event**. When Amazon and Alphabet both shock simultaneously, the reaction exceeds the sum of individual effects because investors reassess whether the entire AI infrastructure cycle's ROI assumptions were too optimistic — not just for one company.

---

### Analyst Consensus Divergence from Market Reaction

Every company in the cycle maintained or improved analyst ratings post-earnings. Alphabet: 60 Buy / 7 Hold / 0 Sell, median target $380–387 vs. stock in the $302–313 range post-sell-off. Amazon: broadly bullish, average target ~$280. This divergence between analyst ratings and stock performance is notable. Analysts are valuing on normalized cash flows with multi-year AI ROI assumptions; the market is pricing near-term FCF compression from capex.

The gap between analyst targets and market prices represents either: (a) analysts are correct and the stocks are undervalued, or (b) analysts have not fully marked down the probability that AI capex cycles produce ROI at the expected magnitude. History suggests (b) is the right concern — large capex cycles have a persistent history of overshooting.

## Strategic Implications

### The State of Enterprise Tech Spending

Enterprise IT budgets are clearly re-expanding after the 2022–2024 optimization pause. The evidence:
- AWS backlog up +22% QoQ to $244B
- Google Cloud backlog up +55% sequentially
- Azure CC growth at +38%, reaccelerating from the 29–33% range in prior quarters
- Microsoft Copilot described as "larger than some established franchises"

The composition of the new spending cycle is structurally different from the 2019–2021 cloud migration wave. Then, the driver was infrastructure lift-and-shift. Now, the driver is AI platform commitment — which involves not just compute spending but model selection, data pipeline investment, fine-tuning, and workforce retraining. This creates deeper switching costs and longer contract duration.

Enterprise IT budgets are not finite; they grow with business complexity. The AI spending cycle does not compete with existing IT budgets so much as it expands the definition of what IT budgets cover. This is the structural bull case for all four companies.

---

### Consumer Technology Demand

Apple's results are the cleanest consumer technology signal of the cycle:
- iPhone +23% YoY was a genuine upside surprise, not just iPhone 17 replacing iPhone 16 — it reflects ASP expansion (iPhone 17 Air) and China demand recovery (+38% in the region)
- Services $30B (+14%) confirms that the installed base of 2.5B+ active devices is monetizing at an accelerating rate
- The guidance of +13–16% revenue growth for Q2 FY2026 implies the cycle continues with no visible demand degradation

Consumer tech is healthy. The iPhone supercycle thesis — that Apple Intelligence would drive the largest upgrade cycle in years — is playing out in the data, even though Apple did not disclose specific AI-driven engagement metrics. The China recovery ($25.53B, +38%) is a direct counter-narrative to the persistent bearish thesis on Apple's exposure to Huawei competitive pressure.

---

### AI's Transition from Investment to Revenue

Q4 2025 is the quarter where AI demonstrably crossed from investment phase to early revenue recognition — though the full transition remains in front of us. Evidence:

**What is confirmed:**
- Amazon Trainium + Graviton: $10B+ ARR, growing triple digits — AI chip revenue is real and scaling
- Google Cloud: $17.7B (+48%), with the acceleration explicitly attributed to AI workload demand
- Azure: AI contribution described as disproportionate to overall cloud growth
- Google Search: +17% with AI Overviews — the feared monetization destruction did not occur

**What remains unconfirmed:**
- Explicit AI revenue line items at any company
- ROI metrics on AI infrastructure investment
- Consumer AI (Apple Intelligence, Alexa+) revenue attribution
- Whether AI advertising CPM improvements are durable or a 1–2 year tailwind

The honest framing: AI has demonstrably created demand pull that is accelerating cloud revenue growth. It has not yet created standalone revenue categories that can be separately valued. The gap between those two statements is where most of the current market debate resides.

**The depreciation cliff (2027–2028)**: The capex being deployed in 2025–2026 will begin converting to depreciation expense in 2027–2028. At $495–640B in combined hyperscaler spending, the depreciation wave — assuming 3–5 year asset lives — will create a structural headwind to reported earnings that is currently absent from most analyst models. Either AI revenue will have scaled sufficiently by then to absorb the depreciation, or earnings estimates for 2027–2028 will need to be revised down. This is the single most important financial watch item from this earnings season.

### Big Tech's Competitive Dynamics in 2026

The Q4 2025 earnings season crystallized four distinct competitive positions for 2026:

**Apple** — Premium consumer platform at the intersection of AI and hardware. The strategic bet: on-device AI inference via Apple Silicon creates a privacy-differentiated, latency-free AI experience that cloud-dependent competitors cannot match. The revenue model: selling premium hardware that enables this experience, then monetizing the platform through Services. Capital intensity: low relative to hyperscalers. Risk: if AI transitions primarily to agentic, cloud-native interactions, Apple's on-device advantage may matter less.

**Microsoft** — The enterprise AI integration layer. Azure OpenAI Service, Copilot, GitHub Copilot, and Dynamics Copilot are the AI features woven into tools that 400M+ commercial users already use daily. The structural advantage: switching costs for enterprise productivity suites are enormous. Revenue model: incremental AI tiers above existing Microsoft 365 commitments. The margin advantage (~43% cloud margins) provides financial flexibility that AWS and GCP lack.

**Amazon** — The infrastructure foundation play. AWS is the largest and most profitable cloud business, now reaccelerating after the optimization pause. Advertising is a structurally growing, high-margin, adjacent revenue stream. The strategic bet is scale: the $200B capex commitment is a bet that being the largest, most reliable cloud provider compounds in a world where AI workload demand is supply-constrained. The Anthropic relationship and Bedrock's model-agnostic positioning differentiates AWS from Azure's OpenAI exclusivity.

**Alphabet** — The fastest-growing hyperscaler with the most to prove. Google Cloud at +48% and the $240B backlog are the bull case. The bear case: advertising, which generates the cash to fund AI, faces long-term structural questions from AI-native search alternatives that Q4 data cannot fully resolve. The $175–185B capex guidance — the largest relative shock of the cycle — reflects a company making an all-in bet on AI infrastructure that it believes is both demand-validated and supply-constrained.

**The structural tension entering 2026**: All four companies are simultaneously competing and cooperating. Amazon and Google jointly fund Anthropic. All three hyperscalers are NVIDIA's best customers and its most serious long-term competitive threat. Apple is both a TSMC competitor for capacity and its largest customer. The lines between collaboration and competition in the AI ecosystem are blurrier than in any prior technology cycle — which makes traditional competitive analysis frameworks inadequate for understanding what Q4 2025 actually signaled.
