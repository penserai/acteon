# Q4 2025 Big Tech Earnings — Market Sector Analysis

> **Reporting window:** January 29 – February 5, 2026 | Apple and Microsoft (Jan 29), Alphabet (Feb 4), Amazon (Feb 5)
> **Analysis date:** March 27, 2026

---

## Overview

The Q4 2025 earnings season for big technology was defined by a single overriding dynamic: every major company beat on revenue and earnings, and the stock market punished two of the four for doing so. The reason is the most consequential capital allocation story in technology in a generation. Amazon, Alphabet, and Microsoft collectively committed to spending **$495–505B in 2026 capital expenditures** — nearly double the prior year's pace and $130–140B above what Wall Street had modeled. Amazon's $200B guide landed 56% above consensus. Alphabet's $175–185B guide was 98% above prior estimates. The operational beats — strong cloud acceleration, record advertising quarters, an iPhone cycle that defied expectations — were treated by markets as secondary noise. The AI infrastructure arms race is the story.

Underneath the capex shock, the underlying fundamentals are the strongest the sector has seen since 2022. Cloud optimization headwinds from 2023–2024 are fully absorbed. Enterprise spending is re-accelerating, led by AI workloads converting from pilot to production. Cloud backlogs at AWS and Google are nearly identical at $244B and $240B respectively — a remarkable convergence that reflects enterprises running multi-cloud AI architectures, not a winner-take-all market. All three hyperscalers described demand as supply-constrained: the bottleneck is data center capacity, not enterprise willingness to pay. This language, appearing independently across competing management teams, is the most reliable demand signal of the cycle.

Apple occupies a structurally distinct position in this landscape. With no cloud infrastructure to build, Apple is a net beneficiary of the AI buildout — capturing upgrade cycle demand from Apple Intelligence-capable hardware, sustaining a record $30B Services quarter, and posting iPhone revenue growth of +23% YoY driven by China recovery and the iPhone 17 cycle. Apple's 48.2% gross margin contrasts sharply with the free cash flow compression implied by hyperscaler capex programs. These are not business quality differences; they reflect entirely different positions in the AI value chain.

The semiconductor sector is the clearest equity beneficiary of this spending cycle. Aggregate big tech AI capex guidance for 2026 approaches $620–640B when Meta is included, creating multi-year demand visibility for TSMC, Broadcom, Micron, SK Hynix, and the networking stack. TSMC sits at the intersection of every major demand vector simultaneously — Apple iPhones, NVIDIA Blackwell GPUs, Google TPU Ironwood, Amazon Trainium3, and Broadcom XPUs all compete for N3/N2 capacity. The SOX index returned +52.0% over the trailing twelve months through the earnings window, closing at a peak of 8,498.10 on February 25, 2026 — the same day NVIDIA reported record quarterly revenue of $68.1B, and its stock fell 5.46%.

## Key Findings

- **Every company beat consensus, but stock performance was split.** Apple (+1.19%) and Microsoft (modestly positive) were rewarded on January 29; Alphabet (-7% to -10%) and Amazon (-8% to -15%) were sold off in the following week — not because of operational misses, but because their capex guidance exceeded Street models by $53–65B each.

- **The AI demand signal is supply-constrained, not demand-constrained.** All three hyperscalers independently characterized their AI infrastructure position in identical terms. Combined 2026 capex commitments of $495–505B (Big Three alone) represent the largest coordinated capital deployment in tech history and establish that enterprise AI adoption is structural, not cyclical.

- **Cloud acceleration is broad-based and reaccelerating.** Google Cloud led at +48% YoY to $17.7B; Azure grew +29% reported (+38% constant currency) to $32.9B; AWS grew +24% to $35.6B — its fastest pace in 13 quarters. The Q4 2025 combined Big Three cloud quarter of ~$86B is up ~30% YoY.

- **Search advertising is not being cannibalized by AI.** Google Search grew +17% to a record $63.1B — its strongest recent quarter — despite AI Overviews being fully deployed. The feared substitution of search ad inventory by generative AI answers has not materialized; AI Overviews are expanding query volume and expanding monetizable commercial intent.

- **Custom silicon is eroding NVIDIA's incremental share.** All three hyperscalers reached material deployment milestones with proprietary AI chips (Google TPU Ironwood, Amazon Trainium3, Microsoft Maia 200). Custom ASIC shipments are projected to grow +44.6% in 2026 versus +16.1% for GPUs. NVIDIA's training dominance is intact; its inference economics are under structural pressure.

- **Cloud backlogs are nearly identical at AWS and Google.** AWS: $244B (+40% YoY). Google Cloud: $240B (+55% sequential). This convergence reflects enterprises running multi-cloud architectures — the zero-sum framing of cloud competition is no longer the correct model.

- **The depreciation cliff is the most important unpriced risk.** The $495–640B in capex being deployed in 2025–2026 will begin converting to depreciation expense in 2027–2028. At 3–5 year asset lives, the earnings headwind is material. Whether AI monetization scales fast enough to absorb it is the central unresolved question of this earnings cycle.

## Company Highlights

### [Apple](companies/apple.md)
Apple delivered its strongest quarterly result in recent memory: $143.8B in revenue (+16% YoY), $2.84 EPS (+19%), and a gross margin of 48.2% — all-time records across every headline metric. The standout was China, which grew +38% YoY to $25.53B, dramatically reversing bearish narratives about Huawei competitive pressure. iPhone revenue of $85.27B (+23%) was well above the $78.65B estimate, driven by the iPhone 17 family including the ultra-thin Air model. Services reached $30B for the first time, sustaining its trajectory as the highest-margin segment and the clearest analog to an AI-leveraged recurring revenue business. Apple provided Q2 FY2026 guidance of +13–16% revenue growth with gross margin guidance of 48–49%, both above prior Street models. Muted stock reaction (+1.19%) on earnings day reflected elevated pre-print expectations — the stock had already partially priced in channel checks — but the results validated Apple's structural positioning as a net beneficiary of the AI cycle without the capital intensity that defines its hyperscaler peers.

### [Microsoft](companies/microsoft.md)
Microsoft's Q4 results were anchored by Azure's reacceleration to +38% constant-currency growth — meaningfully above the +29–33% range of prior quarters — and the milestone of commercial cloud crossing $50B in a single quarter for the first time. Intelligent Cloud segment revenue of $32.9B (+29% YoY) carries operating margins of approximately 43%, the highest in the hyperscaler group. Management described the AI business as "larger than some established franchises," signaling Copilot monetization has crossed a material threshold. Microsoft's $120B+ 2026 capex guidance was received constructively — unlike Amazon's and Alphabet's shocks — largely because Microsoft had telegraphed AI infrastructure investment progressively over prior quarters, allowing the market to absorb the commitment without surprise. The January 29 reporting date (shared with Apple) created a dual-beat anchor that set positive tone for the earnings season heading into hyperscaler week.

### [Alphabet/Google](companies/google.md)
Alphabet's Q4 2025 results were operationally exceptional and market-moving in the wrong direction. Revenue of $113.83B (+18% YoY) and EPS of $2.82 (+30% YoY) both beat consensus, and the company crossed $400B in full-year revenue for the first time in its history. Google Cloud at $17.7B (+48% YoY) was the fastest-growing major hyperscaler and reported a $240B backlog — up 55% sequentially — the product of multiyear enterprise AI commitments. Google Search posted +17% growth despite AI Overviews being fully deployed, effectively resolving the monetization debate in favor of the bulls. Yet the stock fell -7% to -10% in the following week, driven entirely by the FY 2026 capex guidance of $175–185B — $55–65B above the prior Street consensus of ~$119.5B, a near-doubling of what analysts had modeled. CEO Sundar Pichai's "supply constrained throughout 2026" language explained the urgency; the market responded by pricing in the FCF compression. Analyst consensus remained overwhelmingly constructive: 60 Buy / 7 Hold / 0 Sell, with median price targets of $380–387 against a post-sell-off price in the $302–313 range.

### [Amazon](companies/amazon.md)
Amazon's Q4 was the capex cycle's most dramatic data point. Revenue of $213.4B (+14% YoY) beat estimates across every major line; AWS revenue of $35.6B (+24% YoY) was the business's fastest growth in 13 quarters, with operating margins expanding to ~35% and backlog surging to $244B (+40% YoY). Advertising revenue of $21.3B (+23% YoY) confirmed Amazon's position as the world's third-largest digital ad business. Despite all of this, the stock fell -8% to -11% immediately following the February 5 earnings report, with the decline extending to approximately -15% over the following weeks. The cause: 2026 capex guidance of ~$200B against a Street estimate of $146.6B — a $53B+ shock (+56%) that implied negative free cash flow in 2026–2027 at current revenue levels. To fund the buildout, Amazon issued a EUR 14.5B bond in Q4 2025. The Trainium + Graviton combined ARR of $10B+, growing triple digits, was the most specific AI revenue disclosure of the entire earnings cycle. Analyst consensus remained bullish despite the stock reaction, with average price targets around $280.

## Sector Impact

### [Semiconductors](sectors/semiconductors.md)
The Q4 2025 earnings season was the most consequential demand signal for semiconductors in years. Aggregate big tech 2026 AI capex guidance of $620–640B (including Meta) validated the AI infrastructure supercycle thesis across the semiconductor supply chain. TSMC is at the intersection of every demand vector — Apple A19 (N3P), NVIDIA Blackwell (N3), Google TPU Ironwood (N3), Amazon Trainium3 (3nm), and Broadcom XPUs (N3/N2) — all competing for the same advanced-node capacity. The N2 (2nm) ramp, entering high-volume manufacturing in Q4 2025, is the gating factor for next-generation AI silicon from virtually every major designer. Memory is the sector's sleeper story: HBM capacity from SK Hynix and Micron is sold out through calendar 2026, with Micron's gross margins expanding from ~22% in FY2024 to over 50% in FQ1 2026. NVIDIA reported record Q4 FY2026 revenue of $68.1B (+73% YoY) and its stock fell 5.46% — a "buy the rumor, sell the news" reaction reflecting that custom ASIC growth (+44.6% projected in 2026) is outpacing GPU growth (+16.1%). Broadcom is the clearest structural winner: AI revenue of $8.4B in Q1 FY2026 (+106% YoY), guided to $10.7B in Q2, with CEO Hock Tan citing a line of sight to $100B+ in annual AI chip revenue. The SOX index returned +52.0% over the twelve months through the earnings window.

### [Cloud](sectors/cloud.md)
Cloud infrastructure entered a new AI-driven growth phase in Q4 2025, with all three hyperscalers reaccelerating relative to prior quarters. The combined Big Three cloud quarter of ~$86B represents approximately 30% YoY growth, the strongest combined result since 2022. The optimization cycle that compressed cloud growth through 2023–2024 is definitively over; the new spending cycle is AI-led rather than migration-led, and AI commitments carry deeper switching costs — proprietary data pipelines, fine-tuned models, workforce retraining — that create longer contract duration and higher retention. Google Cloud's +48% growth was the headline outperformer, while Azure's +38% constant-currency reacceleration represented the clearest positive surprise for a business at its scale. The near-identical AWS ($244B) and Google Cloud ($240B) backlogs signal that multi-cloud architecture is now the default enterprise posture, not a transition state. Microsoft's superior cloud operating margins (~43% vs. AWS's ~35% vs. Google Cloud's ~20%) reflect software-embedded architecture advantages that favor Microsoft in margin-adjusted return analysis, though Google Cloud's rapid margin expansion trajectory makes it the most interesting medium-term watch item in the sector.

### [Ad Tech](sectors/ad-tech.md)
Digital advertising delivered a strong quarter that was entirely overshadowed by hyperscaler capex announcements. Google's total ad revenue of $82.28B (+13.5% YoY) and Amazon's advertising revenue of $21.3B (+23% YoY) represent two of the strongest quarters in recent history for their respective businesses — yet both companies' stocks declined. Within the advertising results, the structural story is walled-garden consolidation accelerating at the expense of open-web programmatic. Google Search (+17%) and Amazon Advertising (+23%) are both growing faster than total digital ad spending; Google Network (-1.5%) is the clearest proof point that programmatic open-web display is in secular decline. AI is not disrupting advertising — it is reinforcing incumbency. Performance Max, Amazon's relevance models, and Meta's Advantage+ are making dominant platforms more efficient at converting ad spend to outcomes, widening the technical and data gap over independent ad tech. YouTube's apparent deceleration to +8.7% is an election-cycle base effect, not structural; the underlying video advertising business is healthy, with YouTube maintaining its position as the #1 streaming service in the United States by viewing time.

## Cross-Company Themes

The most consequential pattern across all four companies is the **decoupling of operational results from stock performance**. Universal revenue beats — Apple +4.6%, Alphabet +2.4%, Amazon +1.0%, Microsoft +~3–4% — failed to drive positive stock reactions at companies where capital commitments exceeded investor models. This represents a structural break from prior earnings cycles and signals that the market is now pricing AI infrastructure investment as the primary variable, not trailing revenue performance.

Three other convergences stand out. First, all three hyperscalers described demand as supply-constrained using functionally identical language, across competing management teams — an industry-wide data point that validates the AI spending cycle is structural. Second, the near-identical AWS and Google Cloud backlogs ($244B vs. $240B) confirm that multi-cloud has become the default enterprise architecture, making zero-sum competitive analysis inadequate. Third, all three hyperscalers deployed custom AI silicon milestones in Q4 — Google Ironwood, Amazon Trainium3, Microsoft Maia 200 — establishing that the NVIDIA moat is intact for training but under structural pressure for inference.

The key divergence is Apple's structurally different position as the only net beneficiary among the four: no cloud capex commitment, a record gross margin of 48.2%, and a Services business growing at 14% YoY that is the clearest analog to a high-margin AI monetization vehicle in the group.

## Methodology

This analysis is based entirely on publicly available information: earnings releases, earnings call transcripts, analyst research notes, contemporaneous financial press coverage, and publicly disclosed market data. No material non-public information was used or inferred. Financial figures are sourced from the respective company earnings releases where stated; estimates derived from YoY growth trajectories or segment extrapolation are noted as such in the individual reports.

The analysis covers the primary earnings reporting window from **January 29, 2026** (Apple, Microsoft) through **February 5, 2026** (Amazon), with the semiconductor sector analysis extended through **February 25, 2026** (NVIDIA Q4 FY2026). Where Microsoft figures are estimated from segment data, the individual report explicitly notes which figures are confirmed versus derived. All dollar figures are in USD unless noted.
