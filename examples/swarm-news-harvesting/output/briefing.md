# EU AI Regulation Briefing — March 2026

## Executive Summary

The EU AI regulatory landscape is at an inflection point. On March 26, 2026 — today — the European Parliament holds a plenary vote on the "Digital Omnibus," a sweeping simplification package that would delay the most consequential AI Act obligations by 16 months, shifting the Annex III high-risk AI deadline from August 2, 2026 to December 2, 2027. The vote follows rapid institutional alignment: the Council adopted its position on March 13 and the IMCO/LIBE committees voted 101–9–8 on March 18. If passed, trilogue negotiations with the Council will begin immediately.

The Omnibus debate has exposed deep tensions between competitiveness and rights-protection. A coalition of 60+ civil society organizations — including EDRi, AlgorithmWatch, and the Civil Liberties Union for Europe — has labeled the package "the largest rollback of digital fundamental rights in EU history." Simultaneously, Corporate Europe Observatory has documented that 7 of 8 substantive Omnibus changes mirror specific lobbying requests from Google and Microsoft, lending credibility to concerns about regulatory capture. Big Tech collectively spends €151 million per year on EU lobbying, fielding 890 registered lobbyists.

Against this backdrop, enforcement is already moving. The Commission issued a data retention order to X over its Grok AI chatbot in January 2026 and threatened Meta with rarely-used interim measures in February over WhatsApp's exclusion of rival AI providers — forcing a capitulation. US trade pressure has intensified in parallel, with the Trump administration publishing a sweeping pro-deregulation AI strategy on March 20, 2026 and explicitly naming EU enforcement actions as potential triggers for retaliatory tariffs.

## Key Developments

### 1. Digital Omnibus Plenary Vote — March 26, 2026

The European Parliament votes today on a mandate to enter trilogue with the Council on the "AI Act Simplification" portion of the Digital Omnibus. The core proposal would delay Annex III high-risk AI obligations — covering AI systems used in employment decisions, creditworthiness assessment, education, and biometric identification — from the current August 2, 2026 deadline to December 2, 2027, a 16-month extension. Annex I obligations would shift further, to August 2, 2028.

The legislative timeline has moved unusually fast. The European Commission published the Omnibus in February 2026. The Council of the EU adopted a general approach on March 13, 2026. The joint IMCO/LIBE committee vote on March 18 produced a lopsided 101–9–8 result, signaling broad cross-party support in Parliament. Today's plenary vote is therefore expected to pass, opening formal trilogue.

Institutional positions diverge on several specifics that will define the trilogue battle. The most significant gap is on watermarking: Parliament tightened the AI-generated content labeling grace period to November 2, 2026, while both the Commission and Council favor February 2, 2027. Parliament also restored mandatory (rather than advisory) AI literacy obligations and reinstated the Article 49 non-high-risk system registration requirement that the Commission had proposed eliminating. Both Parliament and Council included a new prohibition on "nudifier" AI systems — tools that generate non-consensual intimate imagery — that was not in the original Commission text. The August 2026 deadline remains legally binding until the Omnibus receives final adoption.

The leading MEP voice for simplification, Arba Kokalari (EPP, Sweden), has framed the delay as a competitiveness necessity: "If Europe wants to be competitive, we must increase investment and make it easier to use AI." The nudifier ban sponsor, Michael McNamara (Renew, Ireland), argued the addition reflects "citizens' legitimate expectations." Commission Vice-President Henna Virkkunen has maintained that "simplification does not mean softening our standards" — a formulation civil society groups dispute given the scope of the proposed rollbacks.

### 2. Documented Lobbying Influence on Omnibus Text — Corporate Europe Observatory Analysis

A detailed analysis by Corporate Europe Observatory has mapped specific lobbying requests from Google and Microsoft to specific textual changes in the Digital Omnibus, finding a match in 7 of 8 cases. The finding is significant because it provides named actors, named legal provisions, and documented correspondence — moving the lobbying-capture allegation from general concern to specific evidence.

Google's documented lobbying positions aligned with Omnibus changes to: deletion of ePrivacy Article 5(3) restrictions on tracking, insertion of a "disproportionate efforts" loophole into GDPR Articles 15–22 (limiting data subject rights), and expansion of AI Act Article 10(5) to broaden permitted bias detection data processing. Microsoft's lobbied positions matched changes to: a narrower definition of pseudonymised data (reducing GDPR scope) and more flexible ePrivacy cookie consent rules. Industry associations DigitalEurope, CCIA, and Dot Europe lobbied for abolition of Article 49 registration requirements, recognition of "legitimate interest" as a legal basis for AI training data, and self-assessment exemptions from conformity assessments — all reflected in the Commission's Omnibus draft.

The financial scale of the lobbying effort contextualizes the influence: Big Tech spends €151 million per year on EU lobbying and maintains 890 registered lobbyists in Brussels. Civil society groups have drawn attention to a specific conflict-of-interest: the Parliament's lead rapporteur on the Omnibus, MEP Aura Salla, previously worked as a lobbyist for Meta. A campaign backed by the 60+ organization coalition — including EDRi, AlgorithmWatch, the European Center for Not-for-Profit Law (ECNL), Liberties.eu, and CDT Europe — called for her removal from the role.

The coalition's joint statement characterized the Omnibus as an assault on the GDPR, AI Act, and ePrivacy simultaneously: "This is the largest rollback of digital fundamental rights in EU history." The statement specifically cited the proposed deletion of GDPR data minimization requirements and the weakening of high-risk AI conformity assessment obligations as the most damaging provisions.

Source: https://corporateeurope.org/ (Corporate Europe Observatory analysis, March 2026)

### 3. Meta's Confrontational Regulatory Posture — January–February 2026

Meta has emerged as the most confrontationally positioned major AI company in the EU regulatory environment, simultaneously resisting the GPAI Code of Practice, facing active antitrust enforcement, and running an aggressive lobbying campaign targeting far-right MEPs.

In July 2025, Meta was the only major Western AI provider to refuse to sign the EU AI Office's voluntary GPAI Code of Practice, which 26 providers — including Microsoft, Google, Amazon, OpenAI, and Anthropic — signed. Meta cited legal uncertainty and regulatory overreach. The refusal placed its Llama family of models under closer scrutiny by the AI Office, which is the designated enforcement authority for general-purpose AI systems.

On the antitrust front, the Commission opened a WhatsApp investigation in January 2026 over Meta's policy of blocking rival AI providers from accessing the WhatsApp API. The Commission notified Meta of interim measures on February 9, 2026 — an emergency enforcement tool rarely invoked in EU digital regulation — signaling the Commission viewed the conduct as urgent and likely unlawful. Meta subsequently agreed to allow rival AI providers access to WhatsApp, subject to a fee structure that remains under scrutiny.

Meta's parliamentary lobbying strategy has been equally aggressive. Documented records show that Meta's meetings with far-right MEPs rose from 1 to 38 over the current parliamentary mandate, with specific meetings held in December 2025 focused on the Digital Omnibus. The strategy appears designed to build a blocking minority against stricter amendments. Siemens CEO Roland Busch, speaking in a March 24, 2026 Financial Times interview, captured the industry's broader frustration with EU regulation: "You should not throttle innovation speed for sovereignty. This would be a disaster" — comparing the US economy to a "fast-flowing river" against Europe's "standing water."

### 4. US Trade Pressure and Geopolitical Friction — February–March 2026

The Trump administration has escalated trade pressure on the EU over AI regulation to an extent not seen in prior transatlantic digital policy disputes. The friction has moved from rhetorical to operational, with specific enforcement actions cited as potential tariff triggers.

Vice President J.D. Vance, speaking at the Paris AI Summit in February 2025, set the tone: excessive EU AI regulation would "cripple the industry." By early 2026, the rhetoric had acquired concrete form. The US Trade Representative, Jamieson Greer, explicitly named SAP, Spotify, and Mistral as potential retaliation targets if the EU continued enforcement actions against US technology firms. President Trump separately threatened 25% tariffs on EU technology exports, directly linked to the €4.3 billion Google fine.

On March 20, 2026, the Trump administration published a comprehensive national AI strategy centered on deregulation and American AI leadership — a document framed partly as a counter-model to the EU AI Act's risk-based approach. The strategy has amplified pressure on EU policymakers who argue that regulatory delays are necessary to remain competitive with US firms operating under lighter domestic constraints.

The geopolitical dynamic has given EU industry associations new argumentative leverage. The European technology company lobby has reframed the Omnibus not merely as a competitiveness measure but as a geopolitical necessity — arguing that strict EU AI regulation disadvantages European companies relative to US counterparts who face no equivalent domestic obligations, while doing nothing to constrain the behavior of US companies in EU markets.

Source: US USTR statements and Trump administration AI policy documents, February–March 2026

### 5. EU Parliament Copyright Resolution and AI Training Disclosure — March 10, 2026

On March 10, 2026, the European Parliament adopted a non-binding resolution on AI and copyright by a decisive 460–71 vote. While non-binding, the margin signals strong parliamentary consensus that will inform future binding legislation and enforcement priorities.

The resolution's core demands: AI developers must disclose all copyrighted works used in training datasets; the EU Intellectual Property Office (EUIPO) should establish and manage a machine-readable opt-out register that rights-holders can use to signal that their works may not be used for AI training; and content generated entirely by AI without human creative input should not qualify for copyright protection. The resolution explicitly asserts that EU copyright law applies to AI training regardless of where that training occurred — a direct assertion of extraterritorial reach that parallels the GDPR's approach.

The resolution reflects a sharp industry divide. News publishers, book publishers, and collecting societies expressed support, viewing disclosure and opt-out requirements as minimum necessary protections for their revenue models. AI developers — including representatives of both US majors and European AI startups — opposed the resolution, arguing that dataset disclosure requirements would impose unworkable compliance burdens, expose trade secrets, and chill AI development in Europe.

The practical enforcement pathway for the resolution's recommendations is still undefined, as EUIPO currently lacks jurisdiction over AI training and a new legislative vehicle would be required to create binding obligations. However, the vote gives political momentum to rights-holders seeking to use existing GDPR and copyright enforcement mechanisms in the interim.

### 6. X/Grok AI Act Enforcement Action — January 8, 2026

The Commission issued a formal data retention order to X (formerly Twitter) on January 8, 2026, targeting its Grok AI chatbot's data practices. The order specifically concerned Grok's "Spicy Mode" feature, which had been processing user-generated content under terms the Commission found inconsistent with AI Act transparency and data retention requirements.

The action is notable as one of the first formal Commission enforcement orders under the AI Act directed at a general-purpose AI system's data handling, rather than at a prohibited practice or high-risk classification. It signals that the AI Office is willing to use interim and compliance orders against GPAI systems outside the formal GPAI Code of Practice framework — relevant given that Meta's Llama models are not covered by the Code.

The X enforcement action and the simultaneous Meta/WhatsApp interim measures (February 2026) together represent a significant escalation in active enforcement: two major actions in the first two months of 2026, both targeting US-headquartered platforms, and both occurring before the August 2, 2026 date when the broadest AI Act obligations enter force.

## Outlook

**Immediate (weeks)**

- **Omnibus trilogue**: If the plenary passes today's vote, the Parliament, Council, and Commission enter formal trilogue. The primary battleground will be the watermarking timeline (Parliament: November 2, 2026 vs. Council/Commission: February 2, 2027) and whether AI literacy obligations remain mandatory. Expect intense lobbying pressure during this window.
- **August 2, 2026 deadline**: Regardless of the Omnibus outcome, Annex III obligations remain legally binding until final adoption of any amendment. Companies relying on a delay before completing conformity assessments are taking legal risk. The Commission will face political pressure to provide interim guidance if trilogue extends close to the August date.
- **Meta/WhatsApp**: The interim measures resolution (allowing rival AIs access subject to fees) is likely to face further scrutiny over the fee structure. A formal infringement decision remains possible.

**Medium-term (months)**

- **GPAI Code of Practice finalization**: The Code of Practice for general-purpose AI providers is expected to be finalized in mid-2026. Meta's continued non-participation will determine whether it faces an enforcement proceeding for non-compliance with GPAI transparency obligations.
- **Copyright legislation**: The March 10 resolution will translate into a Commission legislative proposal timeline. EUIPO's mandate expansion and AI training disclosure requirements are the most likely first legislative steps.
- **US tariff pressure**: If the EU continues enforcement against US platforms (particularly any action on Google's AI data practices), the Trump administration has signaled it will respond with trade measures. The USTR's specific naming of SAP, Spotify, and Mistral as retaliation targets is an unusual and escalatory signal.
- **Prohibited practices review**: The February 2, 2026 one-year anniversary of the prohibited practices chapter triggered a mandatory Commission review under Article 112. Results are expected in the second half of 2026 and could trigger enforcement in emotion recognition, predictive policing, or social scoring.

**Structural trends**

The EU's regulatory approach faces a legitimacy challenge from two directions simultaneously: industry and US government pressure arguing it is uncompetitive, and civil society arguing it is being hollowed out through lobbying. The trilogues on the Omnibus will be a critical test of whether the EU can maintain the substantive core of the AI Act — particularly high-risk conformity assessments and GPAI transparency obligations — while making credible concessions on implementation timelines. The outcome will shape the "Brussels Effect" dynamic: whether the AI Act's standards are adopted globally, or whether they are seen as an unstable regulatory environment that firms seek to arbitrage.
