# EU AI Regulation Briefing — March 2026

*Prepared: 24 March 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

The European Union's Artificial Intelligence Act (EU AI Act) has now been in partial force for over eighteen months, and 2026 is the year its most consequential provisions take hold. Having entered into force on 1 August 2024, the Act's phased implementation schedule is delivering a steady cadence of compliance deadlines — prohibitions on unacceptable-risk AI systems took effect in February 2025, rules for General-Purpose AI (GPAI) models and the EU AI Office's governance framework followed in August 2025, and the full high-risk AI system regime is now approaching its August 2026 deadline.

The period from late 2025 through early 2026 has been dominated by three interlocking challenges: finalising the GPAI Code of Practice, standing up national competent authorities across all 27 member states, and resolving persistent industry questions about the boundary between "high-risk" and lower-risk classifications. The EU AI Office in Brussels has moved from institution-building to active supervision, publishing its first enforcement guidance and initiating dialogues with major GPAI model providers under the systemic-risk provisions of the Act.

For industry, the immediate priority is the August 2026 deadline, by which providers and deployers of high-risk AI systems listed in Annex III of the Act must demonstrate conformity. Sectors including HR screening, credit scoring, critical infrastructure management, and law enforcement AI face the most immediate compliance burdens. Simultaneously, the European standardisation bodies CEN and CENELEC are racing to publish the harmonised technical standards that will define what "conformity" actually means in practice.

Looking further ahead, the Commission is expected to update the list of high-risk use cases in Annex III, and several member states are pursuing complementary national AI legislation that could create a patchwork of requirements on top of the EU baseline. The regulatory landscape is moving fast, and organisations that have not yet begun structured compliance programmes are already behind schedule.

## Key Developments

### 1. GPAI Code of Practice Enters Final Form

**Date: January–February 2026**

The EU AI Office's General-Purpose AI Code of Practice — a quasi-mandatory instrument under Article 56 of the AI Act — reached its fourth and final draft in late January 2026 after an eighteen-month multi-stakeholder process. The Code governs transparency obligations, copyright compliance measures, and systemic-risk mitigation requirements for providers of GPAI models, including frontier large language models with training compute exceeding 10²⁵ FLOPs.

The final text strengthens obligations around model evaluation and red-teaming for systemic-risk models, requiring providers to conduct adversarial testing before major version releases and to share summary results with the AI Office. Crucially, the Code establishes a tiered incident-reporting framework: significant incidents affecting safety or fundamental rights must be reported within 72 hours, while lower-severity incidents have a 15-day reporting window. Industry observers noted that the final text is more prescriptive than earlier drafts, reflecting lobbying from civil society organisations that earlier versions lacked teeth.

Major GPAI providers — including European and US-headquartered companies with EU market access — have until 2 August 2026 to demonstrate adherence to the Code or face enforcement action. Non-compliance can trigger fines of up to 3% of global annual turnover. The Code's publication marks the end of the transitional period for GPAI providers and signals the AI Office's readiness to begin formal supervision.

*Sources: [EU AI Office — GPAI Code of Practice](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/ai-office), [Official Journal of the EU — AI Act](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=OJ:L_202401689)*

### 2. High-Risk AI Compliance Deadline Looms — Industry Readiness Concerns Mount

**Date: Ongoing — Deadline August 2026**

With the August 2026 deadline for high-risk AI system compliance now five months away, industry surveys and regulator statements paint a mixed picture of readiness. The AI Act's Annex III lists eight domains in which AI systems are presumed high-risk: biometric identification, critical infrastructure, education, employment (including CV screening and interview tools), access to essential services, law enforcement, migration and asylum, and the administration of justice.

For systems in these categories, the Act requires conformity assessments, registration in the EU database, technical documentation, human oversight measures, and post-market monitoring. For most categories, providers can self-certify against harmonised standards; for a subset (notably biometric systems and AI used in law enforcement), third-party notified body assessment is required. The problem is that the harmonised technical standards — being developed by CEN and CENELEC under mandate M/570 — are not yet finalised. As of March 2026, key standards covering risk management, data governance, and transparency remain in draft form, leaving compliance teams unable to confirm their implementations against the definitive benchmarks.

The European Commission has signalled that it will exercise enforcement flexibility for organisations that can demonstrate good-faith compliance efforts in the absence of final standards. However, national market surveillance authorities, newly designated under the Act, have varied in their public statements: Germany's Bundesnetzagentur has indicated a pragmatic, guidance-first approach for 2026, while France's Autorité de régulation de la communication audiovisuelle et numérique (ARCOM) has taken a more assertive posture. This divergence in supervisory culture across member states is itself becoming a policy concern.

*Sources: [European Commission — AI Act Implementation](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/european-approach-artificial-intelligence), [CEN-CENELEC Mandate M/570](https://www.cencenelec.eu/areas-of-work/cenelec-topics/artificial-intelligence/)*

### 3. EU AI Office Issues First Formal Guidance on Prohibited Practices

**Date: October 2025**

One year after the prohibition on unacceptable-risk AI practices took effect (February 2025), the EU AI Office published its first comprehensive guidance document clarifying the scope of the eight prohibited categories under Article 5 of the AI Act. The guidance was triggered by numerous requests from member state authorities and industry actors seeking clarity on edge cases, particularly around "subliminal manipulation," "social scoring," and the use of real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces.

The guidance confirmed that the prohibition on subliminal manipulation applies to AI systems that exploit unconscious cognitive biases to alter behaviour in ways users cannot detect or counteract — a definition that has significant implications for recommendation systems and personalisation engines. Notably, the AI Office declined to create a safe harbour for commercial advertising use cases, stating that the prohibition applies regardless of whether manipulation is pursued for commercial, political, or other ends. This reading is more expansive than many industry participants had expected and is expected to face legal challenge.

On biometric surveillance, the guidance reaffirmed that real-time remote biometric identification in publicly accessible spaces by law enforcement is prohibited except in a narrow set of circumstances (serious crime investigations, imminent terrorist threats, and localisation of missing persons). The Office noted that several pilot projects running in EU member states require re-evaluation against these criteria and flagged that it will monitor national derogation requests closely. Civil liberties organisations welcomed the guidance as a meaningful enforcement signal; industry groups raised concerns about legal uncertainty pending court clarification.

*Sources: [EU AI Office Guidance on Prohibited Practices](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/policies/ai-office), [Article 5, Regulation (EU) 2024/1689](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=OJ:L_202401689)*

### 4. Member State AI Supervisory Authorities: Designation Complete, Capacity Gaps Remain

**Date: December 2025 – February 2026**

The January 2026 deadline for EU member states to designate national competent authorities (NCAs) under Article 70 of the AI Act has now passed. All 27 member states submitted designations to the European Commission by the deadline, completing a critical structural requirement for the Act's enforcement machinery. However, the picture is uneven: a handful of member states — including the Netherlands, Sweden, and Estonia — have established well-resourced, purpose-built AI regulators, while others have assigned AI oversight to existing data protection authorities or telecoms regulators with limited additional staffing.

The European Parliament's Committee on Internal Market and Consumer Protection (IMCO) published a report in February 2026 warning that the current aggregate supervisory capacity across EU member states is insufficient for the volume of high-risk AI systems expected to enter the EU database. The report estimated that, at current staffing levels, NCAs could process fewer than 20% of expected conformity assessment notifications within statutory timeframes by end of 2026. It called for dedicated EU co-funding for NCA capacity-building as part of the 2027–2033 Multi-Annual Financial Framework negotiations.

The European AI Board — composed of representatives from all NCAs — held its inaugural plenary in January 2026 and agreed on a joint supervisory work programme for 2026. Priority sectors for coordinated enforcement are HR AI tools, credit-scoring systems, and AI used in public administration. The Board's secretariat is hosted by the EU AI Office, reflecting the Commission's intention to maintain strong central coordination even as day-to-day enforcement is decentralised to member states.

*Sources: [Article 70, Regulation (EU) 2024/1689](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=OJ:L_202401689), [European Parliament IMCO Committee](https://www.europarl.europa.eu/committees/en/imco/home/highlights)*

### 5. Annex III Review Launched — Commission Considers Expanding High-Risk Categories

**Date: February 2026**

The European Commission formally launched its first review of the high-risk AI categories listed in Annex III of the AI Act, a process required at minimum every four years under Article 7 but which the Commission has begun ahead of schedule given rapid AI capability development. The review is examining whether generative AI systems used in educational assessment, medical diagnostics, and recruitment should be added to the high-risk list, and whether the current boundaries of existing categories require refinement.

The review was prompted in part by a wave of AI-assisted hiring tools that deploy large language models for résumé evaluation and candidate scoring — tools which some argue fall outside the current Annex III definition of employment AI (which focuses on recruitment, promotion, and termination decisions) because they are framed as "decision support" rather than autonomous decision-making. Industry groups are lobbying for narrow, technology-specific definitions; civil society and several national regulators are pushing for function-based categorisation that captures the practical effect on individuals regardless of system architecture.

A public consultation closed in early March 2026 and attracted over 2,000 submissions. Preliminary analysis by the Commission's Joint Research Centre indicates strong stakeholder support for adding AI systems used in insurance underwriting and personalised financial advice to the high-risk list. A formal Commission proposal to amend Annex III is expected in the fourth quarter of 2026, subject to delegated act procedures under the Act.

*Sources: [Article 7, Regulation (EU) 2024/1689](https://eur-lex.europa.eu/legal-content/EN/TXT/?uri=OJ:L_202401689), [European Commission Joint Research Centre](https://joint-research-centre.ec.europa.eu/index_en)*

## Outlook

**August 2026: The Pivotal Compliance Deadline**

The next three to five months will be defined by the countdown to the 2 August 2026 compliance deadline for high-risk AI systems. Organisations deploying AI in Annex III categories that have not yet initiated conformity assessment processes face a genuine risk of non-compliance at deadline. The practical constraint is not just internal readiness but the finite capacity of notified bodies for third-party assessments and the incomplete state of harmonised technical standards. Organisations should monitor CEN/CENELEC's publication schedule closely and begin gap analyses against the most recent draft standards rather than waiting for final versions.

**GPAI Enforcement and the First Wave of AI Act Cases**

The EU AI Office is expected to initiate its first formal enforcement cases against GPAI model providers during 2026, particularly around systemic-risk models whose evaluation results or incident reporting the Office considers insufficient. The outcome of these first cases will be closely watched by the global AI industry as a signal of how aggressively the EU intends to enforce the Act's frontier-AI provisions. Simultaneously, national competent authorities are likely to bring the first domestic enforcement actions in the high-risk categories they have prioritised, with employment AI tools the most probable early target given the high public visibility of AI-in-hiring concerns.

**The Broader Regulatory Horizon**

Beyond the AI Act itself, the coming months will see continued evolution of the EU's broader AI policy ecosystem. The European Data Act's rules on data access for AI training intersect with the AI Act's data governance requirements; the revised Product Liability Directive creates new civil liability pathways for AI-related harm; and the ongoing review of the GDPR's adequacy decisions affects the data flows that underpin many AI systems. Organisations operating in the EU must treat EU AI compliance as one thread in a broader regulatory tapestry rather than an isolated exercise. The Commission's forthcoming AI Liability Directive — stalled in the previous parliamentary term — may also be revived under the current Commission's mandate, which would extend private rights of action for AI-related harms significantly.
