## Development 1 — EU Council and Parliament Committees Both Back Major AI Act Delay

**Date:** March 13 (Council) and March 18–19 (EP Committees), 2026

**Title:** "Stop the Clock" — EU Moves to Delay High-Risk AI Enforcement by Up to 16 Months

**Key Facts:**
- The EU Council adopted its negotiating position on the **Digital Omnibus on AI** (March 13), and EP committees LIBE and IMCO voted **101–9** (8 abstentions) to adopt a joint report backing the same delays (March 18–19).
- New fixed statutory deadlines replace the original August 2, 2026 deadline:
  - Annex III standalone high-risk systems: **December 2, 2027**
  - Annex I embedded (product-regulated) systems: **August 2, 2028**
  - AI-generated content labeling (Article 50): **November 2, 2026**
- IMCO rapporteur **Arba Kokalari** and LIBE rapporteur **Michael McNamara** (Ireland) co-authored the EP report (A10-0073/2026). Preliminary political agreement reached March 11.
- The Omnibus also softens AI literacy from a hard obligation to a "supportive requirement," removes registration requirements for low-risk systems, and projects **€6 billion in compliance savings by 2029**.
- Justice Commissioner **Michael McGrath** frames changes as "targeted measures" to "harmonise application" — not a reopening of the Act's core protections.
- Trilogue negotiations with the Council are expected through autumn 2026. Parliament full plenary vote was scheduled for **March 26, 2026**.
- Industry Technology Industry Council warned the shortening of the generative AI transparency grace period to three months creates compliance uncertainty; 48 EU trade associations urged rollback of additional requirements.

- https://iapp.org/news/a/meps-reach-preliminary-political-agreement-on-AI-omnibus

**Why It Matters:** This is the single most consequential development of the month. The near-unanimous legislative votes show the EU's willingness to delay its own flagship regulation to protect competitiveness and allow time for standards and guidance to catch up. Every company building for the EU market now has more time — but also more uncertainty during the ongoing trilogue.

## Development 2 — Commission Misses Its Own Guidance Deadline, Exposing an Implementation Crisis

**Date:** February 2, 2026 (deadline missed); ongoing

**Title:** No Guidance, No Standards: EU Commission Fails to Publish Article 6 High-Risk Definitions

**Key Facts:**
- The European Commission missed its **statutory February 2, 2026 deadline** to publish guidance on **Article 6** — the provision determining whether an AI system qualifies as "high-risk."
- Technical harmonised standards from EU standardisation bodies were also not ready; those bodies missed their own **2025 deadlines** and are now targeting end of 2026.
- Commission Deputy DG **Renate Nikolay** (DG CONNECT) acknowledged: *"These standards are not ready, and that's why we allowed ourselves in the AI omnibus to give us a bit more time."*
- Former parliamentary advisor **Laura Caroli** (Brando Benifei's office) was blunter: *"It's just not there, and it is supposed to give clarity."*
- Commission officials planned draft guidelines by end of February 2026 with final adoption possibly March–April 2026.
- The Chamber of Progress urged: *"policymakers should give companies the same breathing room they have afforded themselves."*

**Source URLs:**
- https://iapp.org/news/a/european-commission-misses-deadline-for-ai-act-guidance-on-high-risk-systems
- https://cdt.org/insights/cdt-europes-ai-bulletin-february-2026/

**Why It Matters:** This failure is the stated justification for the entire Omnibus delay process. Companies cannot prepare conformity assessments for rules that have not been defined. It reveals a structural gap: the AI Act passed, but the technical infrastructure to implement it did not. It also provides ammunition to critics who argue EU regulatory ambition is outpacing execution capacity.

## Development 3 — EU AI Office Launches First Formal Investigations into X and Meta Under GPAI Provisions

**Date:** January 8 (data-retention order) and January 16 (formal Ecosystem Investigations), 2026

**Title:** Brussels Effect in Action: AI Office Issues Data-Retention Order Against X, Opens Systemic-Risk Probes into X and Meta

**Key Facts:**
- January 8: The EU AI Office issued a **formal data-retention order against X** requiring it to preserve all internal data relating to its Grok AI chatbot. Regulators are investigating Grok's "Spicy Mode" for allegedly generating non-consensual sexualized imagery and disinformation.
- January 16: The AI Office elevated both investigations to formal **"Ecosystem Investigations"** under the AI Act's systemic risk provisions — the first such actions since GPAI obligations entered force on August 2, 2025.
- **Meta** refused to sign the voluntary GPAI Code of Practice in late 2025, leaving it exposed to fines of up to **7% of global annual turnover** under the Act.
- **X** faces combined AI Act/DSA exposure of up to **6% of global turnover**, compounding the **€120 million DSA fine** already levied against it in December 2025.
- Meta's WhatsApp Business APIs are separately under investigation for allegedly restricting rival AI providers — a competition-framing of AI enforcement.

**Source URLs:**
- https://markets.financialcontent.com/wral/article/tokenring-2026-1-9-the-brussels-effect-in-action-eu-ai-act-enforcement-targets-x-and-meta-as-global-standards-solidify
- https://markets.financialcontent.com/wral/article/tokenring-2026-1-16-the-brussels-reckoning-eu-launches-high-stakes-systemic-risk-probes-into-x-and-meta-as-ai-act-enforcement-hits-full-gear
- https://thinkautomated.io/news/eu-steps-up-2026-tech-enforcement-as-ai-rules-bite

**Why It Matters:** These are the first formal enforcement actions under the GPAI provisions and demonstrate the AI Office functioning as a genuine regulator, not just a standard-setter. The data-retention order is a concrete legal instrument — it creates evidence preservation obligations that could bind future litigation. Meta's refusal to join the voluntary code signals a high-stakes adversarial posture; if sanctions follow, they will set precedent for the entire GPAI enforcement regime.

## Development 4 — US-EU Transatlantic Collision: Trump Administration Threatens Retaliation Over AI Enforcement

**Date:** January 5, 2026 (Irish Times report); ongoing through March 2026

**Title:** Brussels vs. Washington: Trump Tariff Threats and Travel Bans Put EU AI Enforcement Under Geopolitical Pressure

**Key Facts:**
- The Trump administration has explicitly threatened **tariff retaliation** against EU digital regulatory actions targeting US tech companies.
- Secretary of State **Marco Rubio** threatened to bar *"leading figures of the global censorship-industrial complex"* from entering the United States.
- The State Department banned former EU Commissioner **Thierry Breton** from entering the US, citing "censorship" and coercion — a direct diplomatic escalation.
- Big Tech companies took openly hostile positions: **Google** claimed EU AI investigation "risks stifling innovation"; **Apple** demanded Brussels scrap the DMA entirely; **Meta** accused the Commission of trying "to handicap successful American business."
- EU Competition Chief **Teresa Ribera** held firm: *"There have been moments that I have needed to stand up and say: sorry, but we're not going to undo our regulation just because you don't like it."*
- Antitrust scholar **Fiona Scott Morton** (Yale) observed regulators may be calibrating their public posture: *"you're just a little bit more quiet perhaps… there's really no pay-off to making a lot of announcements."*
- The competitiveness gap is stark: the US invests **$60–70 billion/year** in AI vs. the EU's **$7–8 billion**; the US has produced 40 AI foundation models vs. Europe's three.

**Source URLs:**
- https://www.irishtimes.com/technology/big-tech/2026/01/05/eu-readies-tougher-tech-enforcement-in-2026-as-trump-warns-of-retaliation/
- https://markets.financialcontent.com/wral/article/tokenring-2026-1-8-the-transatlantic-tech-collision-washington-and-brussels-face-off-over-ai-enforcement-and-tariff-threats
- https://calmops.com/ai/global-ai-regulation-2026/

**Why It Matters:** Geopolitical pressure is a new variable in EU AI enforcement calculations. The question is whether Brussels will maintain regulatory resolve as US-EU trade tensions escalate, or whether enforcement timelines and aggressiveness will be further softened as a diplomatic concession. The Omnibus delay and competitiveness framing suggest political headwinds are real — but Ribera's public stance indicates the EU is not yet backing down.

## Development 5 — EP Committees Add AI Deepfake/Nudifier Ban and EU Joins Council of Europe AI Convention

**Date:** March 11 (CoE Convention vote) and March 18–19 (deepfake ban adopted), 2026

**Title:** Parliament Adds Explicit AI Deepfake/Nudifier Prohibition; EU Formally Joins Global AI Rights Treaty

**Key Facts:**
- The EP committees voted to **prohibit AI systems from creating realistic images or videos depicting sexually explicit activities or intimate parts of an identifiable person without consent** — a targeted deepfake/nudifier ban added to the Omnibus report (A10-0073/2026).
- MEP **Sergey Lagodinsky** (Germany) championed the provision: *"how much power we are willing to give AI to degrade people."*
- Exemptions exist for platforms with "effective safety measures" — details to be worked out in trilogue.
- Separately, on **March 11**, the European Parliament approved the EU's accession to the **Council of Europe Framework Convention on AI and Human Rights, Democracy and the Rule of Law** — an international treaty placing the EU within a global AI governance architecture.
- The EP also adopted a JURI committee own-initiative report calling for Commission guidance on **copyright and generative AI**, including training data legality and AI-generated content reproduction — adding to the policy workload ahead.

**Source URLs:**
- https://iapp.org/news/a/meps-reach-preliminary-political-agreement-on-AI-omnibus
- https://www.coe.int/en/web/artificial-intelligence/-/eu-parliament-backs-eu-conclusion-of-the-council-of-europe-framework-convention-on-artificial-intelligence
- https://epthinktank.eu/2026/03/13/plenary-round-up-march-i-2026/

**Why It Matters:** The deepfake ban addresses a tangible, widely understood harm and could prove one of the AI Act's most politically durable provisions. The Council of Europe accession matters because it expands EU-style AI governance beyond the EU's 27 member states — the convention is open to non-EU signatories including the UK, US, Canada, and Japan, reinforcing the "Brussels Effect" on global AI norms. Together, these moves show the EU is simultaneously broadening the scope of AI rights protections even while delaying technical compliance timelines.
